1. Executive summary

Research question: How does preventive healthcare access vary across NSW PHNs, and how might cultural diversity help explain differences?

Western Sydney (44.4%) and South Western Sydney (48.1%) have the lowest BreastScreen participation despite among the highest GP Medicare service use (751 and 754 services per 100 people). More GP visits do not automatically mean better screening uptake.

Implication: Policy needs to focus on outreach, language access, and screening pathways, not just GP supply.

3. Methods & definitions

Important: “GP services per 100 people” is NOT “GPs per person”

This counts Medicare-billed GP consultations (MBS items) per 100 residents in a year.

Example: 754 / 100 people ≈ 754 GP appointments billed per 100 residents, roughly 7-8 visits per person on average (including repeat visits). It measures how much the population uses GP services, not how many doctors work in the area.

BreastScreen participation (%)

Percentage of eligible women aged 50-74 who attended BreastScreen Australia in the reporting period. The x-axis on screening charts is always percent (%), ranging from about 44% to 59% across NSW PHNs in this analysis.

5. Findings

Finding 1

Large screening gaps in Western Sydney PHNs

Claim: Western Sydney and South Western Sydney have the lowest BreastScreen participation in this analysis.

  • Evidence: Western Sydney 44.4% (lowest); South Western Sydney 48.1% (2nd lowest); Murrumbidgee 59.4% (highest), a gap of 15.0 percentage points.
  • Why it matters: Lower screening is linked to later cancer detection and worse outcomes at population level.
  • Caveat: Data are PHN-level; CALD breakdown within PHNs would strengthen equity analysis.

Finding 2

High GP use does not guarantee high screening

Claim: PHNs with the most GP Medicare services do not have the highest screening rates.

  • Evidence: SW Sydney ranks 1st for GP services (754.3) but 2nd last for screening (48.1%). Western Sydney ranks 2nd for GP (750.8) but last for screening (44.4%). Murrumbidgee has moderate GP use (616.1) but highest screening (59.4%).
  • Why it matters: Expanding GP capacity alone may not close preventive gaps on its own. Screening still needs dedicated outreach and recall.
  • Caveat: GP counts include all attendances, not screening referrals specifically.

Finding 3

Access barriers may coexist with high service volume

Claim: Population indicators suggest navigation and timeliness barriers that preventive policy must address.

  • Evidence: 29.2% of Australians aged 15+ delayed GP care when needed (ABS 2023-24); 34.6% of NSW residents are overseas-born; Western/SW Sydney show high GP billing + low screening.
  • Why it matters: CALD communities may need translated pathways, bilingual staff, and community-trusted messengers.
  • Caveat: CALD indicators are NSW-wide; PHN-level CALD data recommended for next phase.

6. Policy recommendations

1. Co-design multilingual preventive campaigns

Why: Low screening in diverse PHNs suggests invitation systems may not reach CALD groups.

Who leads: NSW Health, BreastScreen NSW, Western & SW Sydney PHNs, Multicultural NSW.

Partners: Ethnic councils, faith leaders, settlement services, councils, GP networks.

How: Translate eligibility info; community media; fund bilingual educators; track uptake by language where possible.

2. Expand culturally tailored BreastScreen outreach

Why: 15-point participation gap requires removing transport, language, and cultural comfort barriers.

Who leads: BreastScreen NSW, PHN commissioning.

Partners: GPs, women's health services, workplaces, community venues.

How: Mobile screening; bilingual booking; evening/weekend clinics; GP recall prompts.

3. Link screening referrals to high-volume GP pathways

Why: High GP contact without screening follow-up. GP practices are still a natural place to prompt screening.

Who leads: PHNs, NSW Ministry of Health, practice data systems.

Partners: RACGP, practice managers, BreastScreen IT teams.

How: Active recall for eligible women; audit referral-to-screen rates in priority PHNs.

4. PHN equity monitoring with CALD disaggregation

Why: Policy targeting needs local CALD + access data alongside screening.

Who leads: AIHW (data), PHNs (action), ABS (Census).

How: Annual public dashboards combining GP use, screening, and equity indicators.
