## 1. Executive summary

**Research question:** How does preventive healthcare access and engagement vary across NSW Primary Health Networks (PHNs), and how might cultural diversity and migration patterns help explain those differences?

**Headline finding:** Western Sydney and South Western Sydney have the **lowest BreastScreen participation** in NSW (44.4% and 48.1%) despite having among the **highest rates of GP service use** (751 and 754 Medicare GP services per 100 people). More GP visits do not automatically mean better preventive care uptake.

**Why this matters:** Breast screening reduces breast cancer mortality. Gaps in culturally diverse, high-migration areas suggest that **access to GPs and uptake of preventive programs are different problems** — and policy needs to address language, navigation, and outreach, not only GP supply.

**Priority actions:**

| Action | Lead stakeholders |
|--------|-------------------|
| Multilingual prevention campaigns co-designed with communities | PHNs, NSW Health, multicultural peak bodies |
| Targeted BreastScreen outreach in Western & SW Sydney | BreastScreen NSW, PHNs, GPs, community organisations |
| GP-linked screening prompts and recall systems | Primary care networks, Medicare Locals/PHN commissioning |
| PHN equity monitoring dashboards | AIHW, PHNs, NSW Ministry of Health |

## 2. Background: Why cultural diversity context matters

NSW is one of Australia's most culturally diverse states. Preventive programs (like BreastScreen) often assume English literacy, familiarity with the health system, and stable household arrangements. The indicators below describe **population context at NSW level** — they explain *why* we might expect variation in preventive uptake across PHNs, not *what caused* any single PHN's results.

### NSW cultural diversity & access indicators

| Indicator | Value | What it measures | Why it matters for this analysis |
|-----------|------:|------------------|----------------------------------|
| Overseas-born population | 34.6% | Share of NSW residents born outside Australia (ABS Census 2021, table **G09c**) | Overseas-born populations may face language and navigation barriers to screening programs designed for mainstream audiences |
| Recent overseas arrivals | 21.1% | Share of **overseas-born** NSW residents who arrived in **2016–2021** (ABS Census 2021, table **G10**) | A large share of the overseas-born population migrated recently and may have lower awareness of BreastScreen eligibility and booking processes |
| Limited English proficiency | 4.5% | Persons who use another language at home and speak English “not well” or “not at all” (ABS Census 2021, table **G13c**) | Identifies the subset of the population most likely to need translated health promotion and appointment support |
| Family complexity (derived) | 75.1% | Persons in families who are not in a “couple family with no children” (derived from ABS Census 2021, table **G29**) | Households with children, one-parent families, or other structures can affect who receives health messages and who can attend appointments |
| Delayed GP access when needed | 29.2% | Persons aged 15+ in **Australia** who needed to see a GP but delayed or did not go (ABS *Patient Experiences* 2023–24) | Suggests that **reported access barriers exist at population level** even where Medicare service counts are high |

**How to use these indicators:** They frame the equity question. They do **not** replace PHN-level analysis. PHN-specific GP and BreastScreen data appear in Section 4. Census indicators are **NSW-wide**; the GP access indicator is **national** (NSW-specific figures were not used because they were not cited from a published ABS table in this project).

**Data sources for context indicators:** ABS Census 2021 GCP (`GCP_1.xlsx`); ABS *Patient Experiences, Australia, 2023–24*. Full table-level citations are in the References section and `06_References/references.md`.

### 3.2 What “GP services per 100 people” means *(read this before the charts)*

**This is NOT the number of GPs per person.**

The metric counts **Medicare-billed GP consultation services** (MBS items for GP attendances) per **100 people in the population** over a reporting year.

| Example value | Plain-English meaning |
|---------------|----------------------|
| **754 services / 100 people** | Across that PHN, Medicare recorded roughly **754 GP consultations per 100 residents** in the year — on average about **7–8 GP visits per person** (including repeat visits by the same people) |
| **547 services / 100 people** | About **5–6 GP visits per person** on average |

**What it tells us:** How heavily a population uses GP services that are billed through Medicare — a proxy for **primary care utilisation**, not GP workforce supply.

**What it does NOT tell us:** Whether those visits were timely, culturally appropriate, bulk-billed, or connected to preventive care referrals.

### 3.3 What “BreastScreen participation (%)” means

The **percentage of eligible women aged 50–74** who participated in BreastScreen Australia within the reporting period for that PHN geography.

- **44.4%** = fewer than half of eligible women screened  
- **59.4%** = nearly three in five screened  
- National target context: Australia aims for **high population participation** in organised screening; PHN variation shows equity gaps

## 5. Findings *(evidence-backed)*

### Finding 1: Large screening gaps exist in Western Sydney PHNs

**Claim:** Western Sydney and South Western Sydney have the lowest BreastScreen participation in this NSW analysis.

**Evidence:**

- Western Sydney: **44.4%** participation — **15.0 points below** Murrumbidgee (59.4%) and **4.7 points below** the NSW PHN average (~49% unweighted mean across 10 PHNs)
- South Western Sydney: **48.1%** — second lowest

**Why this matters:** Organised breast screening is a key population-level cancer prevention tool. Persistent low participation in Australia's most culturally diverse urban corridors likely contributes to **later-stage diagnosis and worse outcomes** in those communities.

**Caveat:** Participation data reflects eligible women in PHN areas; it does not break down participation by CALD status within the PHN. Further disaggregation would strengthen causal claims about cultural diversity.

---

### Finding 2: High GP utilisation does not guarantee high preventive screening

**Claim:** PHNs with the highest Medicare GP service rates do not have the highest BreastScreen participation.

**Evidence:**

- **Western Sydney** ranks **2nd** for GP services (750.8/100) but **last** for screening (44.4%)
- **South Western Sydney** ranks **1st** for GP services (754.3/100) but **2nd last** for screening (48.1%)
- **Murrumbidgee** has **lower** GP utilisation (616.1/100) but the **highest** screening (59.4%)

**Why this matters:** Policy that only expands GP capacity or MBS activity may **not** close preventive care gaps. Screening requires **program-specific outreach, booking pathways, recall systems, and culturally safe messaging** — not just more GP appointments.

**Caveat:** GP service counts include all Medicare GP attendances, not preventive counselling or screening referrals specifically.

---

### Finding 3: Population-level access barriers may coexist with high service volume

**Claim:** NSW-wide indicators suggest structural access challenges that may affect preventive engagement, especially in diverse communities.

**Evidence:**

- **29.2%** of Australians aged 15+ delayed or did not access a GP when needed (ABS *Patient Experiences* 2023–24; national context indicator)
- Western/SW Sydney PHNs combine **high GP billing rates** with **low screening** — consistent with a pattern where people **use GPs for some care** but **do not convert** that contact into preventive screening uptake
- **34.6%** of NSW residents are overseas-born; **21.1%** of overseas-born residents arrived in 2016–2021 — together these increase the likelihood of language and navigation barriers in mainstream screening funnels

**Why this matters:** Equity policy must address **the quality and navigability of care**, not only service counts. CALD communities may need translated materials, bilingual staff, community champions, and flexible screening locations.

**Caveat:** CALD indicators are NSW-wide; PHN-level CALD proportions would allow stronger geographic matching (recommended next analysis step).

## 6. Policy recommendations *(why, who, how)*

### Recommendation 1: Co-design multilingual preventive health campaigns

| | |
|--|--|
| **Why** | Low BreastScreen participation in high-diversity PHNs suggests information and invitation systems may not reach CALD communities effectively |
| **Who leads** | NSW Health (BreastScreen NSW), relevant PHNs (Western Sydney, South Western Sydney), Multicultural NSW |
| **Who partners** | Ethnic community councils, faith leaders, settlement services (SSI, AMES), local councils, GP networks |
| **How** | Translate eligibility messaging; use radio/community media; fund community educators; track participation by language group where data permits |
| **Success measure** | Participation increase in target PHNs; reduced gap vs state average within 3–5 years |

---

### Recommendation 2: Expand culturally tailored BreastScreen outreach

| | |
|--|--|
| **Why** | Finding 1 shows a 15-point participation gap; mobile and community-based screening removes transport, time, and cultural comfort barriers |
| **Who leads** | BreastScreen NSW, PHN commissioning teams |
| **Who partners** | GPs (referral and recall), Aboriginal Medical Services model (for CALD-adapted outreach), women's health services, workplaces |
| **How** | Mobile screening vans in priority LGAs; bilingual booking support; evening/weekend sessions; GP practice prompts for eligible patients |
| **Success measure** | Uptake in Western/SW Sydney moves toward PHN median; wait times and booking abandonment tracked |

---

### Recommendation 3: Integrate screening referrals into high-volume GP pathways

| | |
|--|--|
| **Why** | Finding 2 shows high GP contact without screening conversion — GPs are a natural touchpoint if systems support recall and referral |
| **Who leads** | PHNs, Primary Sense / practice data vendors, NSW Ministry of Health |
| **Who partners** | RACGP, practice managers, BreastScreen NSW IT integration teams |
| **How** | Active recall for eligible women; MBS-aligned preventive health assessments; audit participation rates by practice in priority areas |
| **Success measure** | Referral-to-screen conversion rate in Western/SW Sydney PHNs |

---

### Recommendation 4: Establish PHN equity monitoring with CALD disaggregation

| | |
|--|--|
| **Why** | Current analysis uses NSW-wide context indicators; policy targeting requires PHN-level CALD and access data linked to screening |
| **Who leads** | AIHW (data), PHNs (local action), ABS (Census products) |
| **Who partners** | Researchers, community advisory groups |
| **How** | Publish annual PHN dashboards with GP utilisation, screening participation, and CALD/access indicators side by side |
| **Success measure** | Transparent public reporting; equity metrics embedded in PHN annual plans |

Preventive healthcare access in NSW is **not uniform** across PHNs. The data show a clear disconnect in Western Sydney: **high Medicare GP activity alongside the state's lowest BreastScreen participation**. Cultural diversity and access-delay indicators suggest **equity-focused outreach and navigation support** — not GP supply alone — should drive policy.
