# GP Utilisation vs Preventive Screening Gap Analysis

**Follow-on analysis to *Preventive Healthcare Access Across NSW PHNs***

---

## 1. Executive summary

**Research question:** Where GP service use is high, is participation in **organised preventive screening** (BreastScreen, bowel, and cervical) also high — or does a gap suggest missed referral and navigation opportunities?

**Headline finding:** **South Western Sydney**, **Nepean Blue Mountains**, and **Western Sydney** combine the **highest Medicare GP utilisation** in NSW with the **lowest average screening participation** across three national programs. The disconnect is **not limited to breast screening** — bowel participation is especially low in Western Sydney (35.6%) and South Western Sydney (36.7%).

**Why this matters:** Policy that assumes “more GP contact = more preventive care” is incomplete. These PHNs may need **program-specific outreach, recall systems, and navigation support** — not only GP supply.

---

## 2. Background

The original NSW PHN report showed that Western and South Western Sydney have high GP billing but the lowest BreastScreen participation. This analysis tests whether that pattern holds across **three organised screening programs**:

| Program | Eligible population (approx.) | AIHW CAN114 sheet |
|---------|------------------------------|-------------------|
| **BreastScreen Australia** | Women aged 50–74 | `BreastScreen, PHN` |
| **National Bowel Cancer Screening Program (NBCSP)** | People aged 50–74 | `NBCSP, PHN` |
| **National Cervical Screening Program (NCSP)** | People with a cervix aged 25–74 | `NCSP, PHN` |

GP utilisation comes from **AIHW PHC 019** (Medicare GP services per 100 population), merged via `MASTER_DATASET.xlsx`.

## 3. Methods

### 3.1 GP–screening gap metric

**GP–screening gap** = GP services per 100 people ÷ mean screening participation (%)

| Value | Interpretation |
|-------|----------------|
| **Higher gap** | More GP activity relative to screening uptake — suggests a **disconnect** between primary care contact and organised prevention |
| **Lower gap** | GP utilisation and screening participation are more aligned |

**Mean screening participation** = average of BreastScreen, NBCSP, and NCSP participation rates for each PHN (latest reporting period in CAN114, July 2023 release).

### 3.2 Quadrant classification

Each PHN is classified relative to the **median** GP rate and **median** mean screening rate:

- **High GP / low screening** — priority gap PHNs  
- **High GP / higher screening** — high contact, better conversion  
- **Lower GP / low screening** — access may be constrained at both levels  
- **Lower GP / higher screening** — lower GP billing but stronger program uptake

### 4.3 Program-specific patterns

**Bowel screening** is the **weakest program** in the highest-gap PHNs:

- Western Sydney bowel: **35.6%** (lowest in NSW)
- South Western Sydney bowel: **36.7%**
- Nepean Blue Mountains bowel: **39.2%**

**Cervical screening** is moderately below state leaders but not as extreme as breast/bowel in Western Sydney — suggesting **some** preventive engagement via self-initiated or GP-linked cervical tests, while **invitation-based** breast and bowel programs lag.

**Contrast:** Northern Sydney has the **lowest gap** (10.9) with the **highest cervical participation** (69.3%) despite mid-range GP utilisation.

---

## 5. Findings

### Finding 1: The GP–screening disconnect is a multi-program problem

**Claim:** High GP utilisation in Western Sydney, South Western Sydney, and Nepean Blue Mountains is **not** matched by participation in breast, bowel, or cervical screening.

**Evidence:** All three PHNs rank in the **top 3** on GP–screening gap; each has mean screening below 48% while GP rates exceed 696/100.

### Finding 2: Bowel screening is an under-discussed equity gap

**Claim:** Bowel participation is **lower than breast** in every high-gap PHN, indicating invitation-based NBCSP pathways may face additional barriers (kit return, literacy, privacy).

**Evidence:** Western Sydney GP-to-bowel ratio = **19.6** (GP services per 100 ÷ bowel %) vs GP-to-breast = **15.7**.

### Finding 3: Nepean Blue Mountains belongs in the priority group

**Claim:** Nepean Blue Mountains was less prominent in the breast-only analysis but ranks **#2** on the multi-program gap — high GP use (751/100) with bowel at 39.2% and breast at 47.4%.

**Evidence:** Gap score 15.8, classified **High GP / low screening**.

---

## 6. Policy implications

1. **Expand beyond BreastScreen outreach** — NBCSP and NCSP need co-designed CALD messaging in high-gap PHNs.  
2. **GP-linked recall for all three programs** — high GP contact is an opportunity for systematic screening prompts, not proof that prevention is working.  
3. **Include Nepean Blue Mountains** in Western Sydney equity planning — shared geography and similar gap profile.  
4. **Monitor program-specific rates** — a composite gap index avoids over-weighting a single screening program.

---

## 7. Limitations

- Screening periods differ slightly across CAN114 tables (latest available column per program).  
- GP services include **all** MBS GP attendances, not preventive or screening-related items specifically.  
- NCSP participation is self-reported program data and may not capture all cervical tests in primary care.  
- Analysis is PHN-level; SA3/LGA disaggregation (Project 1) would sharpen geographic targeting.
